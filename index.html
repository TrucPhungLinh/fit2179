01 Tastes
01 Tastes

What do Australians actually drink?

Australia is often seen as a flat white country, but coffee habits change with age. Older drinkers usually stick with cappuccinos and espresso, while younger drinkers are more likely to choose iced or specialty coffee. The flavour profile sits somewhere between milky comfort and strong coffee taste.

A cold coffee option, from iced lattes to cold brew. Younger drinkers are more likely to choose iced and specialty coffee styles.
02 Benefits
02 Benefits

Why coffee feels like more than a drink to Australian ?

Many people start drinking coffee because it helps them feel more awake, but the habit often becomes more than that. For Australians, coffee can be part of a morning routine, a quick social break, or a small reset during the day. Some benefits are supported by research, while others come from personal experience, but both affect how people value their cup of coffee.

Science says
Alertness is real
Caffeine can make people feel more alert by blocking tiredness signals in the brain. This is why many Australians drink coffee in the morning or during study/work breaks to stay focused.
Culture says
The ritual matters more
71% of Australians say the act of making or ordering coffee is as important as drinking it. Making coffee, ordering it from a café, or drinking it with someone can become a small daily break that helps people reset and connect..
The gap
Belief vs evidence
People often value coffee for social reasons, such as taking a break or spending time with others. These benefits are harder to prove scientifically, but they still strongly influence why people choose to drink coffee.

03 Trade

Where does Australia's coffee come from?

Australia does not grow much coffee commercially, so most beans travel a long way before reaching local cafés. Many start from coffee-growing countries such as Brazil, Colombia, Ethiopia or Vietnam, then arrive through major Australian ports like Melbourne, Sydney, Brisbane and Fremantle

Brazil leads
32% of all imports
Brazil remains Australia's largest single-origin coffee supplier. Melbourne's port receives the biggest share, driven by proximity to major roasters in the inner west.
Vietnam rising
Robusta drives volume
Vietnamese Robusta has doubled its share in a decade, driven by the ready-to-drink and instant coffee segments. It rarely reaches your flat white — but it fills your supermarket shelf.
Ethiopia: specialty
Small but growing
Ethiopian heirloom varietals command a premium in Australia's specialty segment. Yirgacheffe and Sidama are among the most-stocked single origins in Melbourne cafes.

04 What It Costs

How much does a cup cost?

A standard flat white ranges from $4.20 in Melbourne to $4.86 in Darwin. Less than a dollar separates the cheapest state from the priciest — but that gap compounds quickly when you're buying two cups a day, five days a week.

The Melbourne CBD packs more cafes per square kilometre than any comparable district in the country. Lunchtime competition keeps quality high and prices in check.
Specialty triangle
Fitzroy · Brunswick · Collingwood
These three suburbs hold over 60% of Melbourne's specialty roasters. The culture exported globally as "Melbourne coffee" originated in this tight three-kilometre arc.
Regional growth
Geelong & Ballarat expanding
Café counts in Geelong grew 38% between 2017 and 2024 — the fastest growth rate of any Victorian centre outside metropolitan Melbourne.
06 Global
06 On the World Stage

How does Australia compare globally?

By kilograms per person, Australia barely makes the top 40 — sitting under 2 kg while Finland tips 12. Per-capita is a strange ruler though: it punishes countries that drink stronger, smaller cups. Café culture, not volume, is what Australia exports.

Reading the data
Finland: 12 kg
The Nordic record holder. Coffee break ("kahvitauko") is a legal right in many Finnish workplaces — consumed black, all day, in large volumes.
Australia: 1.91 kg
Low per-capita, but that's partly because Australians drink short, strong espresso-based drinks — less liquid, more intensity. By café density, Australia ranks far higher.
China: fastest growth
China's coffee imports nearly doubled to 5.5 million bags in 2024 — driven by urban youth culture and the rapid spread of domestic chains like Luckin Coffee.

The premium end of coffee.

At the top of the market, a kilogram of coffee can cost more than a return flight to Bangkok. Rarity, process and provenance — not flavour alone — drive price. The world's most expensive beans are rarely what Australians actually order.
