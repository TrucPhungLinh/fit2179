01 Tastes
01 Tastes

What do Australians actually drink?

Australia is often seen as a flat white country, but coffee habits change with age. Older drinkers usually stick with cappuccinos and espresso, while younger drinkers are more likely to choose iced or specialty coffee. The flavour profile sits somewhere between milky comfort and strong coffee taste.

D. Iced coffee
The younger pick
A cold coffee option, from iced lattes to cold brew. Younger drinkers are more likely to choose iced and specialty coffee styles.
02 Benefits
02 Benefits

Why does coffee feels like more than a drink to Australians?

Many people start drinking coffee because it helps them feel more awake, but the habit often becomes more than that. For Australians, coffee can be part of a morning routine, a quick social break, or a small reset during the day. Some benefits are supported by research, while others come from personal experience, but both affect how people value their cup of coffee.

Science says
Alertness is real
Caffeine can make people feel more alert by blocking tiredness signals in the brain. This is why many Australians drink coffee in the morning or during study/work breaks to stay focused.
Culture says
The ritual matters more
71% of Australians say the act of making or ordering coffee is as important as drinking it. Making coffee, ordering it from a café, or drinking it with someone can become a small daily break that helps people reset and connect.
The gap
Belief vs evidence
People often value coffee for social reasons, such as taking a break or spending time with others. These benefits are harder to prove scientifically, but they still strongly influence why people choose to drink coffee.
03 Trade
03 Trade

Where does Australia's coffee come from?

Australia does not grow much coffee commercially, so most beans travel a long way before reaching local cafés. Many start from coffee-growing countries such as Brazil, Colombia, Ethiopia or Vietnam, then arrive through major Australian ports like Melbourne, Sydney, Brisbane and Fremantle.

Brazil leads
32% of all imports
Brazil remains Australia's largest single-origin coffee supplier. Melbourne's port receives the biggest share, driven by proximity to major roasters in the inner west.
Vietnam rising
Robusta drives volume
Vietnamese Robusta has doubled its share in a decade, driven by the ready-to-drink and instant coffee segments. It rarely reaches your flat white, but it fills your supermarket shelf.
Ethiopia: specialty
Small but growing
Ethiopian heirloom varietals command a premium in Australia's specialty segment. Yirgacheffe and Sidama are among the most-stocked single origins in Melbourne cafes.

How much does a cup cost?

A standard flat white ranges from $4.21 in Melbourne to $4.86 in Darwin. Less than a dollar separates the cheapest state from the priciest, but that gap compounds quickly when you're buying two cups a day, five days a week.

05 Places
05 Café Concentration

Where is café culture concentrated?

Australia’s café culture is not evenly spread. The largest and darkest bubbles cluster around major capital-city regions, especially Melbourne and Sydney, where high population density, office workers, universities and walkable inner suburbs support more cafés per square kilometre. Smaller but visible café hubs also appear around Perth, Adelaide, Brisbane, the Gold Coast and Hobart, showing that café culture follows Australia’s urban and coastal settlement pattern.

Capital-city clusters

The strongest café concentrations appear around the south-east coast and major metropolitan areas. These cities combine dense neighbourhoods, commuter traffic and social dining habits, making cafés part of everyday urban life.
Density vs total cafés
Bigger bubble ≠ always denser culture
Bubble size shows the estimated number of cafés, while darker colour shows cafés per 100 km². This means a large city may have many cafés overall, but the darkest bubbles highlight compact areas where cafés are especially concentrated.
Beyond Melbourne
Perth · Adelaide · Hobart · Coastal centres
Café culture is not only a Melbourne story. Perth, Adelaide and Hobart also form clear café hubs, while smaller coastal and regional centres show lighter concentrations. The pattern suggests cafés are strongest where tourism, walkability and local social activity overlap.
06 Global
06 On the World Stage

How does Australia compare globally?

Australia may not top the world in coffee consumed per person, with New Zealand and several Nordic or European countries drinking more by volume. But Australia’s coffee story is not about quantity alone. Its culture stands out through espresso-based drinks, dense café scenes and the social habit of meeting over coffee.

Reading the data
Nordic and European leaders
Finland is one of the world’s strongest coffee-consuming countries. The Finnish coffee break, known as “kahvitauko”, is a deeply embedded workplace habit, with coffee often consumed black and repeatedly throughout the day.
Australia: café culture, not volume
Low per-capita, but that's partly because Australians drink short, strong espresso-based drinks, less liquid, more intensity. By café density, Australia ranks far higher.
Big markets are not always big drinkers
Bubble size shows total market volume, not personal habit. Large countries can have huge coffee markets because of population size, even if their per-person consumption is lower than smaller coffee-loving countries.

The premium end of coffee.

At the top of the market, a kilogram of coffee can cost more than a return flight to Bangkok. Rarity, process and provenance drive prices, not flavour alone. The world's most expensive beans are rarely what Australians actually order.
